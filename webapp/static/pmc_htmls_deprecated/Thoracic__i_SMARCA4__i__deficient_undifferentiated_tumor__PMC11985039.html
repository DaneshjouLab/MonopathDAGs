Abstract

Thoracic SMARCA4-deficient undifferentiated tumor (SMARCA4-UT) is characterized by an undifferentiated rhabdoid morphology and SMARCA4 deficiency pathologically. In the 2021 revision of the WHO classification, it was categorized as an epithelial tumor because of its shared genetic background with smoking-related lung cancer.

We describe the case of a 44-year-old man with a rapidly enlarging mediastinal mass observed on chest radiography and CT. The tumor was resistant to radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and immune checkpoint inhibitors, and the patient's survival time was approximately 5 months. Familiarity with the clinical background and imaging findings of thoracic SMARCA4-UT is crucial for early diagnosis and treatment planning.

Keywords: SMARCA4-deficient undifferentiated tumor, CT, Mediastinal mass, Heavy smokers

Introduction

Thoracic SMARCA4 -UT is a rare disease first reported by Le Loarer et al. [1]. It is considered a disease that predominantly affects young male heavy smokers. Compared with typical lung cancer, it progresses extremely rapidly and is often associated with multiple metastases at the time of diagnosis. Approximately 100 cases of SMARCA4-UT have been reported to date. Although there is a paucity of imaging-focused reports, it is frequently described as a large invasive tumor with intense FDG uptake, involving the superior/middle mediastinum, hilum, lung, or pleura [2].

Herein we report the case of a 44-year-old man with a rapidly enlarging mediastinal tumor.

Case report

A 44-year-old man with a 24-year smoking history and no significant medical history was noted to have an enlarged shadow in the right upper mediastinum on chest radiography during physical examination. Two months later, a CT scan revealed a mediastinal mass (Figs. 1A and B). Eleven days after the initial CT scan, the patient visited the emergency department at our hospital because of cough, dyspnea, and throat pain.

Chest radiography at admission showed further enlargement of the right upper mediastinal shadow (Fig. 2). Dynamic CT identified a weakly enhanced tumor in the superior-to-middle mediastinum, which had grown from 5.0 × 5.0 cm to 9.0 × 7.0 cm over 2 weeks compared with the initial CT scan. The right brachiocephalic vein and superior vena cava were highly compressed and narrowed by the mass, and the trachea was left-deviated. Multiple enlarged mediastinal lymph nodes, including those at the right hilum and contralateral regions, were observed (Figs. 3A-E). Laboratory tests revealed slightly elevated LDH (296 IU/L) and CRP (3.29 mg/dL) levels but no other abnormalities. Tumor markers (CYFRA, pro-GRP, CEA, CA19-9) and sIL-2R were all negative.

Mediastinal lung cancer was the primary differential diagnosis, with malignant lymphoma also considered. On the first day of admission, a CT-guided biopsy was performed. Pathology revealed extensive necrosis and clusters of large pleomorphic cells with prominent nucleoli and nuclear atypia (Fig. 4A). Immunostaining revealed positivity for the epithelial markers EMA and AE1/AE3 (Figs. 4B and C), with some low-molecular-weight keratin-positive cells, suggesting an epithelial tumor origin. However, tumor markers that commonly test positive in lung cancer (TTF-1, p40, CK7, ALK) and lymphoma markers (CD3, CD5, CD117) tested negative. Finally, SMARCA4 deficiency was confirmed immunohistochemically (Fig. 4D), leading to a definitive diagnosis of Thoracic SMARCA4-UT on day 12.

During hospitalization, a solitary brain metastasis in the right parietal lobe was identified (Figs. 5A-D), and stereotactic radiation therapy (8 Gy/3 fr.) was performed. Because surgery for the mediastinal tumor was not feasible, radiation therapy (30 Gy/10 fr.) was administered on hospital days 12-25. Starting on day 26, treatment with carboplatin, nab-paclitaxel, and atezolizumab was initiated based on the lung cancer protocols, but the tumor continued to grow regardless. Second-line treatment with docetaxel was commenced on day 88, however by day 107, CT revealed multiple liver metastases and right supraclavicular and upper abdominal lymph node metastases (Figs. 6A-D). The patient passed away due to respiratory failure on day 143.

Discussion

SMARCA4 is one of the tumor suppressor genes encoding the BRG1 protein, a core component of the SWI/SNF (switch/sucrose nonfermentable) chromatin remodeling complex. This complex is broadly categorized into 2 types: one containing SMARCA4 (BRG1) and the other containing SMARCA2 (BRM). It is involved in various processes, including transcription, DNA repair/replication, and chromosomal distribution [3,4]. SMARCA4 deficiency is known to occur in tumors in many different organs, such as the ovary, stomach, and esophagus, as well as in malignant rhabdoid tumors, which frequently affect the kidneys in children, and in hypercalcemic small cell carcinoma of the ovary (SCCOHT) [5]. The prognosis is generally poor in patients with SMARCA4 deficiency. Reports indicate that more than 5% of non-small-cell lung cancers (NSCLCs) show SMARCA4 deficiency [6], although the actual prevalence may be higher than currently recognized.

In the 2021 WHO classification, thoracic SMARCA4-deficient tumors were divided into 2 types: (1) SMARCA4-deficient non-small cell lung carcinoma (SMARCA4-DNSCLC), which exhibits histological similarities to conventional NSCLC, and (2) SMARCA4-deficient undifferentiated tumor (SMARCA4-UT), which shows sarcoma-like histology. This case belongs to the latter category. Previously, SMARCA4-UT was classified as sarcoma. However, the 2021 WHO classification revision reclassified it as a subtype of “other epithelial tumors,” reflecting its shared genetic background with smoking-associated lung cancers. The diagnosis involves identifying SMARCA4 (BRG1) deficiency via immunohistochemistry. Most cases simultaneously exhibit SMARCA2 (BRM) deficiency. Weak and focal keratin expression is frequently observed along with CD34, SALL4, and SOX2 expression. Claudin-4 is often negative [7].

Amandine et al. analyzed 21 patients with SMARCA4-UT and reported that SMARCA4-UT more predominantly occurs in young males in their 30s to 50s, who are heavy smokers. Compared with conventional lung cancer, SMARCA4-UT progresses more rapidly and is often metastatic at diagnosis. Metastases to the brain, liver, lymph nodes, bones, and adrenal glands, are common. The median survival (4-7 months) is poor.

Imaging findings frequently reveal tumors arising in the mediastinum (61.9%) and pleura (28.6%), with a mean tumor size of approximately 12 cm. Calcifications and cystic components were absent, and FDG-PET/CT typically showed intense uptake [2]. Zhou et al. analyzed 35 cases of SMARCA4-UT, reporting that 30.4% were stage III and 69.6% were stage IV at diagnosis. Only 17.1% of patients underwent surgical treatment [8]. Brain metastases, although rare, have been reported in some cases, including 1 patient presenting with upper limb weakness due to multiple brain metastases [9] and another initially diagnosed as NSCLC with brain metastases but later identified as SMARCA4-UT due to subsequent small intestine metastases [10].

Sanai et al. reported that conventional chemotherapy has limited efficacy for SMARCA4-deficient tumors. Platinum-based agents, topoisomerase inhibitors, and taxanes showed poor outcomes, with a 94.7% 1-year mortality rate and a median survival of 6.5 months. Immunotherapy has shown promise for treating SMARCA4-UT, particularly immune checkpoint inhibitors. Tumor shrinkage was observed with nivolumab and pembrolizumab. The results of Large-cohort studies indicate that SMARCA4-deficient tumors demonstrate resistance to chemotherapy, however combining chemotherapy with immunotherapy tends to improve progression-free survival [11].

Reflecting on this case, the patient was a 40-year-old man with a smoking history and a rapidly enlarging mediastinal tumor without calcifications or cystic components, consistent with SMARCA4-UT. At admission, mediastinal lymph node metastases and a solitary brain metastasis were evident, classifying the disease as stage IVB.

The differential diagnosis of a rapidly enlarging mediastinal mass includes mediastinal lung cancer (especially small cell carcinoma), malignant lymphoma, or undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (UPS). Differentiating SMARCA4-UT from mediastinal lung cancer based on imaging is challenging, but SMARCA4-UT often exhibits a sarcomatoid appearance and, relative to its size, tends to lack invasion into surrounding structures. In the case of small cell carcinoma of the lung, the average patient age is 71 years, and the high tumor marker positivity rates of NSE (71.4%-80%) and ProGRP (70%-89.3%) may help in the differentiation [12]. Malignant lymphoma is characterized by relatively high absorption (40-50 HU) on noncontrast CT, rapidly enlarging irregular masses, and lack of vascular invasion with a relatively homogeneous enhancement. UPS of mediastinal origin is rare, but its rapid progression and poor prognosis are similar to those of SMARCA4-UT. The presence of cystic degeneration may serve as a distinguishing feature [13].

Although FDG-PET/CT was not performed because of the immediate initiation of radiation and chemotherapy after CT-guided biopsy, a suspicion of SMARCA4-deficient tumors could have been raised earlier. The diagnosis was ultimately confirmed through SMARCA4 immunostaining. The tumor was resistant to a combination of radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy, and the survival period was approximately 5 months.

Conclusion

We report a rare case of thoracic SMARCA4-UT with a poor prognosis. Immunohistochemical confirmation of SMARCA4 deficiency is essential for diagnosis. Familiarity with the clinical and imaging features of this disease may facilitate early diagnosis and treatment planning.
