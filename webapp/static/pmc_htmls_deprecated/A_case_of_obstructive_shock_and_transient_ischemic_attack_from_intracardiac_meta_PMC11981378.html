Abstract

Introduction:

Cancer-associated stroke (CAS) is a serious complication in cancer patients, particularly those with lung cancer. This case highlights a 76-year-old male with metastatic squamous cell carcinoma of the lung who developed obstructive shock and a transient ischemic attack (TIA) due to intracardiac metastasis.

Case presentation:

The patient, with a history of coronary artery disease, chronic atrial fibrillation, and obstructive sleep apnea, was diagnosed with stage IIIB squamous cell carcinoma of the lung. Imaging revealed a tumor thrombus in the left atrium, extending from the right lower lobe of the lung via the pulmonary vein. The patient presented with hemodynamic instability and a fall. A TIA with right facial droop and left pronator drift developed, which resolved within six hours. Given his poor performance status (ECOG 3), he was started on palliative radiation therapy and discharged to hospice care.

Clinical discussion:

Intracardiac metastasis is a rare complication of lung cancer, leading to severe hemodynamic issues and neurological events like stroke. In this case, the tumor thrombus caused obstructive shock and increased the risk of ischemic stroke. Cancer-associated stroke is more common in patients with thoracic malignancies, underscoring the need for a multidisciplinary approach to manage these complex cases.

Conclusion:

This case highlights the importance of early recognition and a multidisciplinary approach in managing cancer-associated strokes, particularly in patients with advanced lung cancer. Palliative care is essential for symptom management and improving quality of life. Further research is needed to explore targeted treatments for cancer-associated thrombosis.

Keywords: cancer-associated stroke, cardiac metastasis, non-small cell lung cancer, obstructive shock, palliative care, transient ischemic attack

Introduction

Cardiac metastases are tumors that have spread distantly to the different parts of the heart, such as tumors that cause intracavitary neoplastic thrombi or damage the heart cavities[1]. Four different pathways exist for malignant neoplasms to potentially reach the heart: direct extension, lymphatic system transmission, bloodstream dissemination, and intracavitary diffusion through the pulmonary veins or the inferior vena cava[2]. Depending on the particular histotype, lung carcinomas have different rates of metastatic spread. Cardiac spread is seen in 26% of instances of adenocarcinoma, 23.4% of squamous cell carcinoma and 21.2% of cases of undifferentiated carcinoma[1].

The histology of lung cancer and stage of disease are important factors that determine the risk of thrombotic complications. Adenocarcinoma is now known to be the most thrombogenic tumor among non-small cell lung cancers (NSCLC), which account for over 80% of all primary lung malignancies[3].

An uncommon but dangerous side effect of cancer is called a “cancer-associated stroke” (CAS), which happens when a tumor or its metastases enter or compress brain blood arteries, causing a stroke. During the first six months after being diagnosed with cancer, the risk of an ischemic stroke increases threefold on average for those with cancer[4]. Patients with metastases and those with aggressive malignancies, such as pancreatic and lung tumors, are more at risk. When compared to strokes in people without illness, strokes in people with cancer are frequently more severe, have higher death rates, and are more likely to recur[4].

We report the case of a 76-year-old male with metastatic non-small cell carcinoma of the lung presenting with intracardiac metastasis and a transient ischemic attack. Given his poor nutritional and performance status, he was deemed unsuitable for systemic therapies, prompting a referral for palliative radiation to manage his symptoms effectively.

Case presentation

A 76-year-old male with a history of coronary artery disease status post coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), chronic atrial fibrillation, and obstructive sleep apnea was initially diagnosed with locally advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). The lung malignancy was identified after being evaluated for cough, chest discomfort, loss of appetite and weight loss over the last 3 months. This was confirmed with endobronchial ultrasound-guided fine-needle aspiration, which demonstrated stage IIIB (T4N2Mx) metastatic non-small cell lung carcinoma of squamous cell histology.

A CT angiogram (Fig. 1), performed 3 days later, revealed the presence of a large hypermetabolic mass in the right lower lobe extending into the mediastinum with enlarged hypermetabolic subcarinal nodes. The irregular mass measuring approximately 8.7 × 4.6 cm, was accompanied by partial atelectasis of the right middle and lower lobes and small to moderate low-density right pleural effusion. More concerningly, it infiltrated the left atrium via the right lower pulmonary vein. There is a tumor thrombus in the left atrium measuring 3.4 × 6.3 cm, interfering with the mitral valve function. An echocardiogram showed that the mass was invading into the left atrium. Given the patient’s poor nutritional and performance status (Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group Performance Status Scale-3), the oncology department recommended against surgery or chemotherapy. He was advised to undergo palliative thoracic radiation for symptomatic relief and adequate pain management.

The patient presented to the ER eight days after the initial diagnosis, after falling from standing height. On admission, the patient appeared lethargic, tachycardic with irregular rhythm, decreased breath sounds on the right, and tender right upper quadrant due to rib fractures. His heart rate was 110 bpm, blood pressure 96/60 mmHg, respiratory rate 22, and saturation of 100%. The patient was initially treated for hypotension with IV fluids. Laboratory and Radiological investigations were carried out and discussed by the trauma team to assess the patient’s status. CT of the head was negative for any acute intracranial processes. CT of the abdomen and pelvis was also performed, indicating mildly displaced rib fractures. In addition, a right kidney interpolar cyst with new areas of internal hyperdensity was assessed and, after discussion, suspected to represent benign cysts. No other new presentations were appreciated. The laboratory results over the days the patient was admitted are shown in Table 1.

The laboratory values for the patient highlight key changes relevant to his clinical condition. Hemoglobin increased from 9.6 to 10.4 g/dL and hematocrit from 28.3% to 31.4%, indicating a positive response to treatment, which is important given his poor nutritional status. The platelet count rose from 128 K/uL to 170 K/uL, suggesting recovery from thrombocytopenia, while white blood cell counts fluctuated between 16.29 and 20.71 K/uL, possibly indicating infection or inflammation. Sodium levels decreased from 129 to 127 mEq/L, and potassium increased from 3.7 to 4.7 mEq/L, necessitating careful monitoring due to his cardiac history. Glucose levels improved significantly from 146 mg/dL to 111 mg/dL, which is beneficial in a palliative care setting. Additionally, elevated PT and INR values point to potential coagulopathy, requiring close attention, especially given the risk of thromboembolic events in a patient with atrial fibrillation. Overall, these laboratory changes provide important insights for managing the patient’s condition. The patient was then admitted for palliative radiotherapy and management of mixed hypovolemic and obstructive shock.

The patient was evaluated 2 days post-ER admission following the development of symptoms suggestive of a transient ischemic attack (TIA) with right facial droop and left pronator drift. He was conscious and oriented during this event. CT-Angiography did not show any large vessel occlusion or any acute intracranial process. The deficits resolved in 6 hours; however, the patient still had ongoing tachycardia and uncontrolled atrial fibrillation. The patient was discharged to hospice care for symptom palliation.

In reflecting on his treatment journey, the patient expressed fear and uncertainty following his diagnosis of advanced non-small cell lung cancer, valuing clear communication with his healthcare team about treatment options. He appreciated the support from palliative care, which alleviated anxiety and prioritized his quality of life, emphasizing the importance of being involved in decision-making throughout his journey.

Discussion

Compared to primary heart tumors, metastatic cardiac malignancies are far more common. Local invasion of the heart by primary lung tumors is common, and they can spread by the lymphatic system, the circulation, or both[1]. On the other hand, it is extremely uncommon for a thrombus in the pulmonary vein to extend to the left atrium. Vascular intrusions into the intrapulmonary veins and intraatrial masses can produce large emboli that impede outflow and compromise circulation. Notably, individuals with this kind of extension have reported experiencing syncopal episodes, peripheral artery occlusion[1].

Metastasis to the heart is more common than primary cardiac tumors due to factors like the heart’s rich blood supply, anatomical proximity to the lungs, and slower blood flow, which facilitates tumor adherence. NSCLC gene mutations, including EGFR and ALK, promote angiogenesis and hematogenous metastasis, increasing the risk of cardiac metastasis (CMT). Cardiac tamponade, a potential complication of CMT, results in hemodynamic compromise due to increased pericardial pressure, leading to obstructive shock and potentially life-threatening outcomes[5].

Another consequence of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in individuals with lung cancer, especially adenocarcinoma, is pulmonary embolism (PE). The occurrence of PE in cancer patients surpasses that in individuals with other illnesses, and the incidence rates escalate significantly over time. Cancer has been related to the complex interactions that occur between substances produced by the malignant tumor cells that promote blood coagulation and fibrinolysis. According to research by Blom and colleagues[3], people with adenocarcinoma are more likely than those with squamous cell carcinoma to have VTE. There is little information on PE cases that happen after lung cancer diagnosis. According to Lee et al[6], PE can happen 3 to 4 months after diagnosis, and cases can last up to ten years. There are benefits to using VTE prevention, including lower incidence of PE and higher rates of postoperative survival[7]. The danger of hemorrhagic strokes and the possibility of postoperative intrathoracic hemorrhage are the drawbacks of VTE prevention, especially for patients who have epidural catheters inserted for pain control.

Cardiac tumors, though rare, can cause significant neurologic complications. Primary cardiac tumors, particularly atrial myxomas, are more likely to cause embolic events like stroke. Metastatic cardiac tumors, on the other hand, typically result in valvular disruption, arrhythmias, and diminished cardiac output, which can indirectly affect neurological function. Both primary and metastatic cardiac tumors can lead to cerebral metastases[8].

There is growing acknowledgement of the link between Acute Ischemic Stroke (AIS) and cancer. It is expected that the incidence of cancer-related AIS will rise annually in tandem with the significant increase in the number of cancer patients. According to recent studies, there is a link between stroke and certain cancer kinds in both men and women. Remarkably, the correlation was strongest for intrathoracic and respiratory tumors[9]. There are a few case reports in the literature regarding lung cancer metastasizing to the heart and tumor thrombi causing ischemic stroke.[10-16] Many cohort studies have found an increased incidence of Acute Ischemic Stroke (AIS) in patients with lung cancer, colon cancer, rectal cancer, pancreatic cancer, cancer of the central nervous system, stomach cancer, and ovarian cancer[17-19].

Differentiating stroke types in cancer patients is challenging due to overlapping clinical features and the effects of hypercoagulability and treatment complications. In patients with atrial fibrillation, the use of anticoagulation therapy must be carefully balanced to mitigate the risk of ischemic stroke while considering the increased likelihood of bleeding from tumor-related vascular issues. A multidisciplinary approach is essential to navigate these complexities and optimize patient management.

Circulating tumor extracellular vesicles were shown to be critical for cancer-associated thrombosis and tumor growth, especially in patients with adenocarcinoma, according to Bang et al[20]. A study by Lin et al described CYFRA-211 as a separate risk factor for individuals experiencing acute ischemic stroke caused by large artery atherosclerosis. There is a 7% increase in the likelihood of AIS attacks for every 1 ng/ml concentration increase. Through the release of cytokines, procoagulants linked to cancer, and other tissue factors, cancer cells can activate the body’s coagulation mechanism. Furthermore, cancer cells can interact with platelets, which results in the production of chemicals that aid in blood coagulation, such as 5-HT, arachidonic acid, and ADP. Globally, cancer and acute ischemic stroke (AIS) are now the two main causes of mortality among the aged[21].

Currently, there is little data to suggest that individuals with lung cancer and those without it should get significantly different treatments for acute ischemic stroke. Therefore, unlike a gastrointestinal malignancy, lung cancer should not be considered a strict no-go for intravenous thrombolysis. In terms of prevention, physicians need to weigh the benefits and risks of an anticoagulant treatment plan carefully and use caution when putting it into practice. Low Molecular Weight Heparins (LMWHs) are still the recommended first option. The patients’ life expectancy may be shortened and their general quality of life may be worsened by the association between AIS and lung cancer.

The increasing prevalence of cancer-associated stroke (CAS) underscores the need for comprehensive care in patients with advanced malignancies and complex comorbidities. A multidisciplinary approach – integrating oncology, cardiology, palliative care, and trauma teams – is essential to effectively address the multifaceted challenges these patients face. Prompt evaluation of neurological symptoms, such as transient ischemic attacks (TIA), is crucial for optimizing outcomes and preventing further complications. Moreover, prioritizing palliative care enhances the quality of life by ensuring effective symptom management and addressing psychosocial needs. Overall, integrated management strategies that consider the complexities of cancer and comorbid conditions are vital for improving care delivery for patients facing serious health challenges.

Conclusion

This case highlights the complexities of managing obstructive shock and transient ischemic attack (TIA) resulting from squamous cell carcinoma of the lung metastasizing to the heart, which significantly complicates treatment planning. The occurrence of acute ischemic stroke in cancer patients underscores the need for innovative strategies to address the underlying prothrombotic state associated with malignancies. We believe that a tailored, multidisciplinary approach involving oncology, neurology, and cardiology is essential for effectively managing such complications and improving patient outcomes. This case also emphasizes the critical role of palliative care in enhancing quality of life when treatment options are limited. Moving forward, further research into the mechanisms of cancer-associated thrombosis is warranted to develop targeted interventions that can better support patients with similar presentations. Our experience suggests that individualized management strategies can greatly benefit those facing the dual challenges of cancer and cardiovascular complications.
